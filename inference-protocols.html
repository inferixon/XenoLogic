INFERENCE PROTOCOLS // CORE LOGIC

These protocols summarize the reasoning patterns behind XenoLogic. Strange lifeforms change their behavior. Logic does not.

Implication and deduction

If a report states "If A, then B," our first job is to respect the direction of the claim. The protocol allows valid chains such as modus ponens and modus tollens, but it rejects shortcuts that feel plausible without being proven.

  • Modus ponens - if A leads to B, and A happened, B follows.
  • Modus tollens - if A leads to B, and B did not happen, A did not happen.
  • Hypothetical syllogism - if A leads to B and B leads to C, A can lead to C.

Quantifiers and scope

Reports often move between "all," "some," and "none." The scope matters. A statement about one glorp cannot automatically become a law for every glorp in the archive.

  • Universal claims - rules that apply to every member of a class.
  • Existential claims - evidence that proves at least one case exists.
  • Null claims - rules that eliminate the whole class from a condition.

Disjunction and contradiction

Some reports use inclusive or. Others use exclusive or. A contradiction appears when a report claims both a statement and its negation at the same time.

  • Inclusive or - A or B, and sometimes both.
  • Exclusive or - A or B, but not both.
  • Contradiction - the report cannot support A and not-A at once.
  • Insufficient information - no forced conclusion without proof.

Known fallacies

XenoLogic is designed to catch elegant mistakes. The most dangerous errors are the ones that sound clever while still breaking the report.

  • Affirming the consequent - B happened, therefore A caused it. Not valid.
  • Denying the antecedent - A did not happen, therefore B could not happen. Not valid.
  • Predicate substitution - changing the property while pretending the logic stayed the same.
  • Class substitution - replacing one species with a similar but different one.
